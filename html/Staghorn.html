Staghorn coral Facts

  • Length: Individual branches can grow up to 2 meters (6.6 ft) in diameter
  • Weight: Not typically measured by weight; forms dense, branching colonies
  • Lifespan: Colonies can live for decades if undisturbed
  • Diet: Photosynthesis via symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) and plankton capture
  • Habitat: Shallow tropical reef environments in the Caribbean and western Atlantic
  • Behavior: Fast-growing reef-building coral; forms dense thickets important for fish habitat
  • Reproduction: Asexual fragmentation and annual sexual spawning events
  • Conservation status: Critically Endangered (IUCN Red List)
  • Population: Drastically reduced; up to 98% decline since the 1980s

Why is it endangered?

Staghorn coral is critically endangered due to a combination of disease outbreaks, rising ocean temperatures, and physical destruction from storms and human activity. Coral bleaching caused by climate change is a major threat, weakening or killing the coral's symbiotic algae. Additional threats include pollution, overfishing (which disrupts reef balance), and coastal development. Although restoration efforts such as coral gardening and marine protected areas are underway, climate resilience and large-scale habitat protection are essential for long-term survival.
